Project Abstract

This document presents Phase 1 research and conceptual design for a hybrid electric mini tractor engineered to compete in Ontario Truck & Tractor Pulling Association (OTTPA) events. Our series-hybrid architecture combines a diesel generator with battery storage and dual electric motors to deliver 1500 Nm of torque per wheel—sufficient to lift the tractor's front end during competition pulls.

Key Innovation

Our hybrid drivetrain uses a diesel generator to power a battery pack, which supplies electric motors. This architecture provides instant torque delivery and superior performance compared to traditional mechanical drivetrains, while enabling modular upgrades and precise control.

Hybrid System Architecture

Our series-hybrid configuration eliminates the mechanical connection between engine and wheels, providing superior control, efficiency, and performance characteristics.

1
Generation

Diesel engine rotates a 20 kW alternator at constant optimal speed, maximizing fuel efficiency and reducing emissions compared to variable-speed operation

2
Storage

Battery pack (48V, 100Ah) buffers energy and supplies peak loads during critical pull phases, enabling power delivery beyond generator capacity

3
Control

Dual motor controllers regulate torque delivery using field-oriented control (FOC), providing precise response to throttle input with 500A capacity per channel

4
Propulsion

Rear-mounted electric motors drive wheels through reduction gearing, delivering instant torque from 0 RPM with approximately 10:1 reduction ratio

Torque & Power Analysis

Torque requirements determine motor selection and power system capacity. We calculate wheel torque from traction force and wheel geometry.

Fundamental Torque Equation

T = F × r

where T = torque, F = traction force, r = wheel radius

For a target traction force of 6000 N per wheel with 0.25 m effective radius (18.4 × 16.1 tire specification):

T = 6000 × 0.25 = 1500 Nm
Performance Significance

This torque level provides sufficient force to lift the tractor's front end—a key indicator of pulling capability. Front-end lift transfers weight to the rear wheels, maximizing traction at the crucial moment when sled resistance peaks.

Power Requirements

Continuous power demand depends on pull duration and resistance profile. The diesel generator must sustain base load while the battery handles transient peaks. A 20 kW generator provides approximately 27 horsepower—adequate for sustained pulling without battery depletion.

Control System Design

The control architecture manages power flow between generator, battery, and motors while optimizing for performance and component protection.

Project Reflection

This project integrates mechanical, electrical, and control engineering into a cohesive system design. I learned to balance competing constraints—weight versus strength, power versus efficiency, complexity versus reliability—while meeting strict regulatory requirements.

Working with hybrid powertrains revealed how electric propulsion fundamentally changes vehicle dynamics. The instant torque response and precise control enable performance impossible with mechanical drivetrains, while the modular architecture allows iterative improvement without complete redesign.

The interdisciplinary nature of this project mirrors real-world mechatronics engineering, where system-level thinking matters as much as component-level expertise. Understanding how to calculate torque requirements, balance power generation with storage, and organize complex engineering documentation has prepared me for the technical depth required in my future studies.

This experience directly supports my goal of studying mechatronics engineering. The project demanded technical depth in multiple domains while requiring professional documentation and systematic design methodology—skills essential for any engineering career. The ability to translate abstract concepts into concrete specifications, while maintaining awareness of practical constraints, represents the core competency of successful engineering practice.
